Exercise 1 (Convergence of Gaussian random variables).

Let (Xn,n≥1) be a sequence of Gaussian random variables with Xn∼𝒩⁢(mn,σn2) (i.e. with mean value mn and variance σn2), defined on the same probability space. Suppose that Xn converges to a random variable X in L2 as n→∞.

  1. 1.

    Prove that, the two limits m:=limn→∞⁡mn and σ2:=limn→∞⁡σn2 exist. Moreover, the limit X is a Gaussian random variable X∼𝒩⁢(m,σ2).

  2. 2.

    Prove that, limn→∞⁡Xn=X also holds in Lp with any 1≤p<∞.

Solution.

A stronger statement holds: Let (Xn,n≥1) be a sequence of Gaussian random variables with Xn∼𝒩⁢(mn,σn2) (i.e. with mean value mn and variance σn2), defined on the same probability space.

  1. (i’)

    Suppose that Xn converges to a random variable X in distribution as n→∞. Then the two limits m:=limn→∞⁡mn and σ2:=limn→∞⁡σn2 exist. Moreover, the limit X is a Gaussian random variable X∼𝒩⁢(m,σ2).

  2. (ii’)

    Suppose that Xn converges to a random variable X in probability as n→∞. Then limn→∞⁡Xn=X also holds in Lp with any 1≤p<∞.

Proof of (i’)  By the convergence in distribution, we have the convergence of characteristic functions:

limn→∞𝔼[exp(iθXn)]=limn→∞exp(imnθ-σn22θ2)=𝔼[exp(iθX)]=:ϕX(θ),∀θ∈ℝ. (1)

We continue the proof in a few steps:

  • •

    The sequence (σn2) is bounded. Suppose by contradiction that a subsequence σnk2→+∞ as k→∞. By (1), we have for every θ∈ℝ, the modulus satisfies

    |ϕX⁢(θ)|=|𝔼⁢[exp⁡(i⁢θ⁢X)]|=limk→∞⁡|exp⁡(i⁢mnk⁢θ-σnk22⁢θ2)|=limk→∞⁡exp⁡(-σnk22⁢θ2)=0.

    In particular, the function ϕX is not continuous at θ=0. This is in contradiction to the fact that ϕX⁢(θ) is a characteristic function.

  • •

    The sequence (mn) is bounded. Suppose by contradiction that a subsequence mnk→+∞ as k→∞ (the case that a subsequence converges to -∞ is similar).

    Note that ℙ(Xnk≥mnk)=1/2 (by Gaussian distribution). For any small ϵ>0, choose M>0 large enough such that ℙ(|X|≥M)<ϵ. Then by convergence in distribution (use Portmanteau Theorem):

    ϵ>ℙ(|X|≥M)≥lim supk→∞ℙ(|Xnk|≥M)≥lim supk→∞ℙ(Xnk≥mnk)=1/2,

    where the second inequity holds since mnk>M for all k large enough. We have obtained an absord inequality.

  • •

    Now we can extract a subsequence (nk) such that

    limk→∞σnk2=:σ2,limk→∞mnk=:m.

    Then by (1):

    𝔼⁢[exp⁡(i⁢θ⁢X)]=limk→∞⁡exp⁡(i⁢mnk⁢θ-σnk22⁢θ2)=exp⁡(i⁢m⁢θ-σ22⁢θ2).

    This shows that X∼𝒩⁢(m,σ2).

  • •

    We have

    limn→∞σn2=σ2,limn→∞mn=:m.

    If one can extract a subsequence that converges to a different pair (σ~2,m~), then (1) implies that X∼𝒩⁢(m~,σ~2), which is in contradiction to the previous step.

Proof of (ii’)  Since the sequences (σn2) and (mn) are bounded, by a simple calculation of moments of Gaussian, we have

supn⁡𝔼⁢[|Xn|p]<∞,∀p≥1.

As a consequence,

supn⁡𝔼⁢[|Xn-X|p]<∞,∀p≥1.

Fix any p≥1 and let Yn:=|Xn-X|p. Then by the above display:

supn⁡𝔼⁢[|Yn|2]=supn⁡𝔼⁢[|Xn-X|2⁢p]<∞.

That is (Yn) is bounded in L2, thus uniformly integrable. Since Yn→0 in probability by assumption, we also have Yn→0 in L1 due to the uniformly integrability. ∎
